This tool captures vehicle location, acceleration, and participant responses directly in the field without needing direct interaction, which greatly enhances the relevance and scope of the data.   • Learning and Adaptation: Adapted to a hands-on learning approach, teaching myself React Native and navigating complex project troubleshooting without traditional guidance.

🧩 Background
Traditional transportation research often relies on lab-based data collection, where direct interaction with participants is necessary. This approach limits the scope and environmental relevance of the data, particularly when studying driving behaviors and passenger experiences in the wild. To overcome these constraints, we are developing "Data to Go," a versatile mobile app designed for in-situ data collection. This tool automates the gathering of vehicle location, acceleration, and participant responses, eliminating the need for direct interaction with participants. It can also be customized for different research studies, such as paratransit evaluations, in-vehicle passenger motion sickness, and teenage driver behavior, etc.
✅ What I Did
  • Pilot Testing on Web and Mobile App
    To determine the best platform for our final product, we developed and tested preliminary demos on both a mobile app and website. We tested Android Web vs iOS Web, Android Web vs Android App, iOS Web vs iOS App, and Android app vs iOS app. These tests were designed to assess each platform’s capability in obtaining the required data, and also to test performance differences between libraries and operating systems. Based on the pilot test results comparing the mobile app demo vs. website demo, the mobile app platform was selected primarily based on the app’s ability to gather continuous and stable data when screen locks or when users are using other apps. This was a fun experience for me in that this is my first time doing a comparative test in computer science and I got that chance to improve what I developed based on performance from different trials.

  • Acceleration Measure
    Among the three main types of data we handle—GPS location, acceleration, and survey responses—measuring acceleration has proven to be the most challenging. Here are a few hurdles we've encountered:

    • Device Variability:
      Acceleration data can vary significantly between different operating systems and even between models of the same brand.

    • Background Data Collection on Android:
      Android imposes strict limitations on background data collection, necessitating continuous user notifications via a foreground service. Unfortunately, there are no existing packages that collect sensor data in the background, which requires additional custom development.

    • Gravity Interference:
      The initial package we used to collect acceleration data included gravitational effects in the results. To address this, I consulted with Robotics students at our institute and ultimately implemented a sensor fusion solution using the Madgwick filter, which combines data from the gyroscope, accelerometer, and magnetometer to isolate and remove the influence of gravity.

    This phase of the project was both demanding and rewarding, pushing us through a cycle of trial, error, and eventual success. It was incredibly satisfying to develop a solution that effectively addressed these complexities.
🏆 Next Steps
During my summer internship, I developed the core functionality of our app, focusing on optimizing the data collection process, which is now fully operational. Here are the next steps we plan to take for next semester to deploy the app for real use:
  • Database Management:
    We will implement a robust database management system using AWS to ensure data integrity and security.
  • Multimodal Prompts:
    To enhance user engagement, we will introduce a variety of prompts that utilize auditory/verbal feedback and haptic vibrations.
  • Customizable UI for Researchers:
    An additional user interface will be created to allow researchers to add study-specific customizations easily.
These enhancements are designed to improve the functionality and user experience of our app, making it more versatile and researcher-friendly.
💡 Reflections
AI and Software Engineering: Tools, Not Replacements
With the increasing popularity of AI and ChatGPT, there’s a lot of talks on AI replacing entry-level SWE jobs. I was kind of on board with that idea until this project gave me a whole new perspective. For example, I tried using GPT to help debug while working with the Expo Location package. I told GPT to set the data collection frequency to every second. Unexpectedly, no data was collected at all, and I couldn't figure out the problem. After a full day of troubleshooting, I discovered that the frequency parameter GPT suggested didn't even exist—it was a made-up.

This was a big wake-up call. While GPT is great for simple tasks or code snippets, it’s not so good with the complicated stuff. It’s still crucial for software engineers to verify the accuracy of AI-generated outputs. This experience showed that AI tools like GPT are just that—tools. They can boost our productivity but are not a substitute for the critical thinking and problem-solving skills that are essential in software development.

Through this project, I learned to balance the use of AI and my own thinking and coding. I also learned the importance of thorough troubleshooting and debugging in managing large, complex projects, and found that maintaining clean and well-organized code is crucial in identifying and resolving bugs effectively.
Embracing Self-Directed Learning
This project really strengthened my ability to dive into the unknown and pick up new skills quickly. Growing up, I was accustomed to the traditional learning pattern where I'd listen to lectures, absorb all the concepts from teachers, and then apply what I learned on homework. However, I've found that computer science doesn't always follow this pattern. Often, there’s no one to teach you exactly what to do, and you have to figure things out using resources like GitHub, YouTube, StackOverflow, and various documentation.

Before this project, I had never developed a mobile app. I taught myself React Native from scratch. This was both challenging and rewarding. I discovered that learning through actual implementation sticks with me far more than just studying the concepts theoretically. I've come to appreciate this hands-on learning approach, and I’m pleased to find that I can now learn new things more quickly and am more open to exploring unfamiliar territories.
